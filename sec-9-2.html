Activity 9.2.3. The Midpoint Formula.

(a)

Plot the points \((-5,3)\) and \((3,-9)\text{,}\) and draw the line segment joining them.

(b)

Draw horizontal and vertical line segments to make a right triangle.

(c)

Now start at \((-5,3)\) and move half the horizontal distance and half the vertical distance to \((3,-9)\text{.}\) What are the coordinates of the new point?

(d)

The new point is called the midpoint of the segment joining \((-5,3)\) and \((3,-9)\text{.}\) It is half-way between \((-5,3)\) and \((3,-9)\text{.}\)

How can we find a formula for calculating the coordinates of the midpoint? When we calculate the average of two numbers, we find a number that is half-way between them. For example, if your test scores are 70 and 90, your test average is

\begin{equation*} \text{average} = \dfrac{70+90}{2} = \dfrac{160}{2} = 80 \end{equation*}

Activity 9.2.5. Circles.

A circle is the set of all points in a plane that lie at a given distance, called the radius, from a fixed point called the center. We can use the distance formula to find an equation for a circle.

(a)

The center of a circle is the origin, \((0,0)\text{.}\) The distance from the origin to any point \((x,y)\) on the circle is \(r\text{.}\)

  1. Use the distance formula to write and equation about the circle.

  2. Square both sides of your equation and simplify, to obtain the equation for a circle of radius \(r\) centered at the origin.

Activity 9.2.6. Standard Form.

The standard form for a circle is useful because we can see the center and the radius right in the equation, so we can graph the circle.

(a)

State the center and radius of the circle \(~(x+3)^2 + (y+2)^2 = 16\text{.}\)

(b)

Graph the circle on the grid.

(c)

Expand the left side of the equation, and write it in general quadratic form:

\begin{equation*} ax^2 + bxy + cy^2 + dx + ey + f = 0 \end{equation*}

(d)

To convert an equation to standard form we complete the square. Follow the steps to convert the equation
